# 6. Real-World Benchmarking: Starlink and Kuiper

We use real-world operator data as an external benchmark rather than as a direct validation target. The purpose of the benchmark is diagnostic: if optimized portfolios differ from observed deployments, the difference may reveal constraints that are missing from the mathematical model. In this case, the comparison shows that population-weighted demand alone is not sufficient to explain real gateway placement. Operator deployments also reflect licensing, sanctions exposure, terrestrial fiber access, peering strategy, national rollout priorities, and market-access feasibility.

The Starlink benchmark is the stronger comparison because Starlink is already operational at scale. We use 250 operational gateway locations from a compiled public dataset that combines FCC IBFS records, public gateway-status reporting, geocoding, and PeeringDB information. The unconstrained WorldPop portfolio is far from the observed Starlink footprint: the median distance from an operational Starlink gateway to its nearest model-selected site is 7,154 km, and only 7.6% of Starlink gateways lie within 1,000 km of a model-selected site. After imposing the geopolitical feasibility constraint, the WorldPop portfolio becomes much more consistent with Starlink's deployed footprint: the median nearest-gateway distance falls to 881 km, and 57.6% of operational Starlink gateways lie within 1,000 km of a model-selected site. This does not mean the model is predicting Starlink's exact network. Rather, it shows that the added feasibility constraint moves the population-weighted optimization in the same broad direction as real operator siting.

The Kuiper benchmark must be interpreted more carefully because Amazon's network is still in build-out. Unlike Starlink, Kuiper does not yet provide a mature global gateway footprint. Its public gateway evidence is therefore a partial deployment signal, not an equilibrium network design. The FCC gateway sample is especially U.S.-weighted, so it should be compared primarily as a jurisdiction-specific build-out snapshot rather than as a global target. In that U.S.-focused sample, Kuiper sites are closer to the uniform baseline portfolio than to the unconstrained WorldPop portfolio. This does not contradict the WorldPop result; it reinforces the central point that real deployments are constrained by where an operator is licensed and ready to build. During early build-out, Amazon's observed gateway choices reflect regulatory approvals, launch sequencing, and U.S. infrastructure readiness as much as global population demand.

Taken together, the Starlink and Kuiper comparisons motivate the geopolitical constraint added in this report. Starlink provides the mature-network benchmark showing that the geopolitical-constrained WorldPop solution aligns much better with an operational gateway footprint. Kuiper provides a build-out-phase benchmark showing why observed gateway samples should not be treated as full global optima. The real-world comparison therefore improves the model rather than merely decorating it: it reveals that demand modeling determines where the optimizer wants to build, while geopolitical and backhaul feasibility determine where an operator can build.
